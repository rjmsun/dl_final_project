\begin{abstract}
This report investigates the design and evaluation of denoising autoencoders (DAEs)
for 1D synthetic time-series signals. We implement and compare four neural
architectures---Fully Connected (MLP), 1D Convolutional (CNN), Long Short-Term Memory
(LSTM), and a Transformer-based encoder-decoder---trained to reconstruct clean signals
from inputs corrupted by four noise types: Gaussian noise, random masking, impulse
(salt-and-pepper) noise, and sinusoidal interference. We sweep over bottleneck
dimensions $z \in \{16, 32, 64\}$ and report reconstruction quality using Mean Squared
Error (MSE) and Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) improvement. Additionally, we evaluate
out-of-distribution noise generalization. Our results show that CNN is the most
consistent performer across all noise types, while LSTM and Transformer architectures
achieve the highest peak SNR improvement on impulse noise, and MLP lags substantially
across all conditions.
\end{abstract}

%==============================================================================
\section{Introduction}
%==============================================================================

Denoising is a fundamental challenge in signal processing: given a corrupted observation
$x_{\text{noisy}}$, the goal is to recover the original clean signal $x \in \mathbb{R}^L$.
Denoising Autoencoders (DAEs) address this by learning a mapping $f_\theta$ such that:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{x} = f_\theta(x_{\text{noisy}}) = \text{Dec}(\text{Enc}(x_{\text{noisy}})),
\end{equation}
where the encoder compresses the input into a low-dimensional bottleneck $z \in \mathbb{R}^d$
and the decoder reconstructs the signal from $z$. The parameters $\theta$ are learned by
minimizing the MSE reconstruction loss:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}\left[\|x - f_\theta(x_{\text{noisy}})\|_{2}^2\right].
\end{equation}

A central design question is whether the inductive bias of the architecture---local
convolutions, recurrent state, or global attention---affects how well the model exploits
temporal structure for denoising. We explore this across four distinct architectures and
four noise corruption types, with a systematic sweep over the bottleneck compression
ratio and a dedicated out-of-distribution generalization experiment. This experimental
design follows the requirements of ECE 685 Final Project 2, which asks for denoising
autoencoder comparisons on 1D time-series signals under multiple noise models using MSE
and SNR-based evaluation \citep{ece685_project_handout}.

%==============================================================================
\section{Dataset and Noise Models}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Synthetic Signal Generation}

Our dataset consists of synthetic 1D signals generated procedurally. Each signal of
length $L = 256$ is a superposition of multiple sine wave components with randomized
amplitudes (sampled uniformly from $[0.3, 1.0]$), frequencies (from $[1.0, 12.0]$ Hz),
and phases. To increase structural diversity, a chirp component (frequency sweep) is
appended with 35\% probability. All signals are peak-normalized to $[-1, 1]$. We use a
split of 2{,}048 training samples, 512 validation samples, and 512 test samples.

\subsection{Noise Models}

We implement four noise models to simulate a range of real-world signal distortions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Gaussian Noise:} Additive white noise $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^{2})$
          with $\sigma = 0.15$.
    \item \textbf{Random Masking:} Contiguous segments of length 4--16 samples are zeroed out
          until approximately 15\% of the signal is removed, simulating packet loss or sensor
          dropout.
    \item \textbf{Impulse Noise:} Salt-and-pepper spikes of amplitude $\pm 3.0$ are injected at
          5\% of randomly chosen timesteps, simulating sensor malfunctions.
    \item \textbf{Sinusoidal Interference:} A 5 Hz periodic tone is added to simulate background
          electrical hum or line noise.
\end{itemize}

We also train models on a \textit{combined} condition (\texttt{both}) that applies Gaussian
and masking noise simultaneously.

\subsection{Multilayer Perceptron (MLP)}

The MLP baseline treats the entire 256-sample sequence as a flat vector. The encoder
consists of three fully-connected layers ($256 \to 128 \to 64 \to z$) with ReLU
activations, and the decoder mirrors this structure symmetrically ($z \to 64 \to 128 \to
256$). With only 86K parameters, this is the most compact model but has no notion of
temporal ordering or locality.

\subsection{1D Convolutional Autoencoder (CNN)}

The CNN encoder uses two strided 1D convolutional layers (kernel size 4, stride 2) to
progressively halve the temporal resolution while increasing channel depth, followed by a
linear projection to the bottleneck $z$. The decoder uses transposed convolutions to
upsample back to length 256. This architecture captures local temporal patterns
efficiently and is computationally fast.

\subsection{LSTM Autoencoder}

The LSTM encoder is a bidirectional LSTM that processes the sequence timestep-by-timestep,
projecting each hidden state to the latent dimension $z$. The decoder is a unidirectional
LSTM that autoregressively reconstructs the signal. The bidirectional encoder allows the
model to leverage both past and future context, which is particularly useful for filling
in masked regions.

\subsection{Transformer Autoencoder}

The Transformer model uses multi-head self-attention (4 heads, 2 encoder layers, 2 decoder
layers) with sinusoidal positional encodings. Self-attention directly computes pairwise
dependencies between all timestep pairs in $O(L^2)$, enabling the model to capture global
structure without the vanishing gradient limitations of RNNs. The hidden dimension is
automatically rounded to be divisible by the number of attention heads.

\section{Experiments and Analysis}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

All models were trained for 20 epochs using the Adam optimizer (learning rate $10^{-3}$,
batch size 64). We conducted a full sweep of
$4\ \text{architectures} \times 3\ \text{bottleneck sizes} \times 5\ \text{noise modes}
= 60$ training runs. The primary evaluation metrics are test MSE and SNR improvement,
defined as:
\begin{equation}
    \text{SNR}(x, \hat{x}) = 10 \log_{10}\!\left(\frac{\|x\|_{2}^2}{\|x - \hat{x}\|_{2}^2}\right).
\end{equation}
SNR improvement is computed as $\text{SNR}(x,\hat{x}) - \text{SNR}(x, x_{\text{noisy}})$,
so positive values indicate the model reduces noise relative to the corrupted input.

Several key patterns emerge. First, the \textbf{MLP performs the worst} across nearly all
conditions, as it cannot exploit any temporal structure. Notably, MLP achieves negative
SNR improvement on masking noise ($-0.35$ dB), meaning its reconstructions are actually
noisier than the input. Second, for \textbf{stationary Gaussian noise}, CNN achieves the
highest SNR improvement (+7.95 dB) at $z=32$, likely because local convolutional filters
are well-suited to smoothing stationary additive distortions while adding minimal model
complexity (287K parameters). LSTM is competitive (+7.88 dB) but at 8$\times$ the
parameter cost. Third, for \textbf{impulse noise}, CNN (+27.4 dB), LSTM (+24.4 dB), and
Transformer (+24.8 dB) all dramatically outperform MLP (+8.2 dB). The large absolute
gains are partly attributable to the very low input SNR of impulse-corrupted signals,
making reconstruction easier in relative terms. Fourth, for \textbf{random masking}, CNN
again leads (+4.7 dB) while the Transformer shows near-zero improvement (+0.14 dB),
suggesting that global self-attention does not effectively infer dropped segment content
from the remaining signal.

\subsection{Effect of the Latent Dimension}

Figure~\ref{fig:bottleneck} illustrates how the bottleneck dimension $z$ affects test
MSE. Across all models, larger $z$ consistently reduces reconstruction error, as looser
compression retains more signal information.

\begin{figure}[h]
\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{fig_bottleneck.png}
\end{center}
\caption{Effect of bottleneck size $z \in \{16, 32, 64\}$ on test MSE, per architecture.}

Notably, CNN and LSTM maintain low MSE even at $z = 16$, whereas MLP and Transformer
degrade more steeply under tight compression. This indicates that convolutional and
recurrent feature representations achieve more information-efficient encodings than flat
or attention-based ones on this dataset.

\subsection{Generalization Across Noise Levels}

To assess out-of-distribution robustness, we trained models on Gaussian noise
($\sigma = 0.15$) and evaluated them at seven noise intensities
$\sigma \in \{0.05, 0.10, 0.15, 0.20, 0.25, 0.30, 0.40\}$.
Figure~\ref{fig:generalization} shows SNR improvement as a function of $\sigma$.

\begin{figure}[h]
\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{fig_generalization.png}
\end{center}
\caption{Generalization experiment: SNR improvement vs.\ Gaussian noise intensity $\sigma$.
All models were trained at $\sigma = 0.15$ (dashed line) and evaluated across the full range.}
\label{fig:generalization}
\end{figure}

All models achieve the highest SNR improvement at low $\sigma$ (high input SNR), where
signals are only mildly corrupted and reconstruction is straightforward. As $\sigma$
increases beyond the training distribution ($\sigma > 0.25$), all models degrade. LSTM
and CNN maintain positive SNR improvement most robustly, while MLP degrades most steeply
on out-of-distribution noise levels.

\section{Conclusion}
%==============================================================================

We implemented and systematically compared four denoising autoencoder architectures for
1D synthetic time-series signals across four noise types and three bottleneck compression
ratios. Our key findings are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{CNN} provides the most consistent denoising performance across all noise
          types, is computationally efficient (287K parameters), and is the best
          single-model choice for unknown noise environments.
    \item \textbf{LSTM and Transformer} achieve the highest peak SNR improvement on
          impulse noise, leveraging temporal context to suppress large-amplitude spikes,
          but at substantially higher parameter cost.
    \item \textbf{MLP} fails to exploit temporal structure, achieving near-zero or
          negative SNR improvement on masking noise, underscoring the importance of
          architectural inductive biases for signal denoising.
    \item \textbf{Larger bottleneck} dimensions consistently reduce MSE; convolutional
          and recurrent representations are more information-efficient and robust at small
          bottleneck sizes.
    \item \textbf{Noise generalization} degrades gracefully as test $\sigma$ diverges
          from the training value, with CNN and LSTM maintaining positive SNR improvement
          most robustly across the evaluated range.
\end{itemize}

Future directions include variational autoencoders (VAEs) for smoother and more
structured latent manifolds, perceptual or frequency-domain loss functions to better
capture signal quality, and evaluation on real-world datasets such as ECG recordings,
accelerometer data, or audio.
